The data for these charts above comes from the database https://public.emdat.be. This database also contained information about earthquakes and landslides, but we decided to not include these disasters because climate change should not affect these disasters. Regarding the above charts, the database only contains information on number of people affected and cost of damages for some of the disasters, thus we decided not to include these in measuring the impact and extent of these natural disasters.

Information

Within the next century, sea levels are expected to rise by 30 meters (100 feet) if global mean temperature rises by 2 degrees Celsius (the target under the Paris Agreement). Therefore, we want to show how the world's will change in this time period.
Sea level rise: USA.
Sea level rise: Asia.
Notice that large population centers near the coast will be underwater, meaning there will be a huge cost of rebuilding infrastructure more inland. (Building infrastructure is one of the largest contributors of climate change.) Additionally, some countries, such as Bangladesh (near India) will be almost completely submerged. These mostly submerged countries will be the source of an increasing number of refugees in the 21st century, causing a huge humanitarian crisis. Furthermore, currently a substantial proportion of agricultural output in the world is also near the ocean. In this map, much of this land will also be submerged and more will be unsuitable for agriculture. Therefore, there will also be a food shortage and prices of these daily necessities will increase dramatically. As an example, Florida is the biggest producer of oranges for the US. However, in the map, Florida is almost completely submerged, meaning prices for oranges and products containing oranges will spike.
When sea water encroaches further inland, many sources of freshwater/drinking water will also be engulfed, worsening the already bad clean water crisis. Not to mention, the more saline water will also disrupt freshwater environments, many of which are places of recreation. Furthermore, the seawater near the coasts will become less saline, causing farmed fish populations to decline in size. Increased acidity of seawater also causes shellfish populations to decline. As demand for seafood increases, supply will decrease, driving prices even higher.
